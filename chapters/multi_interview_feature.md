# How to: write a multi-interview feature

When people talk about "features" the most likely genre of feature they are talking about is the **multi-interview feature**. This is a feature which focuses not on a particular person, but on an *issue*, by speaking to a range of people connected to that issue. 

Examples might include:

* A problem they are either affected by, or exploring
* A key event they were involved in
* An experience they have had, or are researching
* An industry they work in, or are experts on
* A type of expertise they share

For example, ['‘I never want you around your grandchild’: the families torn apart when adult children decide to go ‘no contact’'](https://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/2024/nov/09/the-families-torn-apart-when-adult-children-decide-to-go-no-contact) explores the issue of estrangement by talking to different people involved in the issue: those directly affected, and experts in the issue. 

Another example of a multi-interview feature, ['Leaky ceilings and sinking floors: inside St Helier hospital where staff fear for patients'](https://www.theguardian.com/uk-news/2024/dec/30/inside-st-helier-hospital-staff-patient-safety) speaks to those affected by the hospital, as well as those with responsibility for it. 

[‘Nigel Farage feels real’: why young British men are drawn to Reform](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2025/mar/08/nigel-farage-feels-real-why-young-british-men-are-drawn-to-reform?CMP=insidesaturday_email) seeks to answer that question by speaking to a range of people at a political rally, but also people tackling issues around masculinity.

A more creative example is [‘Nobody’s going out!’ Why is Britain’s nightlife in such decline – and can anything save it?](https://www.theguardian.com/music/2026/may/05/why-is-britains-nightlife-in-decline) which combines first person reporting (a journey around Birmingham at night) with multiple interviews: people that they meet ("party veterans" and young clubbers), an expert (a "veteran promoter") and an industry representative (from the Night-Time Industry Association, NTIA). 

Planning a multi-interview involves the following:

1. Identify the issue you are going to explore
2. Conduct background research to identify potential themes/sections
3. Choose the sections your story will have (the themes)
4. Identify a list of a range of potential interviewees you can speak to
5. Organise the interviews
6. Plan the interviews
7. Conduct the interviews, including photos and other media
8. Draft the story
9. Edit the story
10. Publish and distribute!

## 1. Identify the issue you are going to explore

A multi-interview feature takes time - not just yours, but the readers' as well, because it will be long. At least 800 words, often 1,200-1,500 words, and sometimes longer.

So you need to pick an issue which justifies that time and effort. 

Look for issues which are either timeless, or still likely to be topical by the time your story goes live. In the examples above, families torn apart is timeless, while hospitals falling apart, the decline of nightlife and the question of why people are drawn to Reform are all topics likely to be topical for some time. 

The best way to come up with these ideas is to follow as much news as possible: every news report is a potential starting point for a follow-up feature that asks "why" (why are people joining Reform? Why is the UK's nightlife in decline?), "how" (how do people become homeless? How did our rivers become polluted?), or "what is the impact" (what is the impact of crumbling hospitals or children breaking contact?)

## 2. Conduct background research to identify potential themes/sections

Once you've identified the issue - don't leap into doing interviews. First decide what the 'themes' might be in your feature. 

Background research will help you understand what the talking points are, what the history is, and who the key figures are. It will stop you making basic errors once you contact people and start to talk to them - interviewees will lose patience quickly with a journalist who hasn't done their research.

Make a note of any potential interviewees as you go along. 

## 3. Choose the sections your story will have (the themes)

Because of its length, a multi-interview feature is often divided into sections, each of which explores a different theme in the issue. 

An issue might have many themes you *could* explore, so you'll need to decide which ones you *will* explore. In order to 

Typical sections include what it's like to experience the issue; causes; trends; and what is being done about the issue. 

Another approach is to organise the feature geographically, with each section focusing on a different country or area. 

Each section should answer a particular question - sometimes a question raised by the previous section. The feature about Reform, for example, starts with a section speaking to members which introduces the problem of toxic masculinity, so the next section explores attempts to address that, before a section about rightwing populism. Having focused on men, the next section explores the question of women in far-right parties. Final sections often explore questions about the future: in this case, how likely it is that Reform would win an election - before returning to where we started (the members). 

A geographically-organised feature does this much more simply: "What is happening in the UK?" "What is happening in France?" and so on. A chronologically-organised approach might follow a process, such as becoming homeless or undergoing medical treatment: "What happens then? What are the problems with this stage?"

The nightlife feature raises questions in a different way, by using Birmingham's nightlife scene. At first they try Digbeth, then they try Broad Street, then the Gay Village. The simple question of "Where can we go for a night out?" leads to questions around "Why is the nightlife so bad?" which takes us to the final sections that explore what promoters and industry representatives say. At the end, again, it returns to where we started (finding somewhere to dance in Birmingham).

## 4. Identify a list of a range of potential interviewees you can speak to

Once you know your sections, you can start to list potential interviewees. You'll need a list of people who, between them, can answer all the questions your sections aim to answer.

Some useful approaches to take include the following:

* Different perspectives: experts, people with power to do something, people directly affected, and representatives of people who might be involved. This is the approach taken by all the examples above.
* The same 'type', multiple times: this tends to work best for features which are about "what it's like" to experience something. For example if your feature is about what it's like to be adopted, you might interview 3-5 people who have been adopted themselves. 
* Different levels or steps in a process or system: for example, if your feature was following what happens to your waste, you might interview a refuse collector, the CEO of a company that processes waste (or an industry representative of those companies), a resident or campaigner living near the waste processing company, the regulator that is supposed to enforce the laws around waste, charities campaigning for change, and so on. 

Use the [PEER technique](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2026/03/09/peer-a-technique-for-brainstorming-interviewees-and-story-sources/) to make a list of people in the four main categories that are used by journalists:

* Power: politicians, regulators, law enforcement, bosses, etc.
* Expertise: academics, researchers at charities, award-winners etc.
* Experience: those directly affected (often reached through charities)
* Representatives: unions, industry bodies and others who can speak for a wider group of people

Look at the examples to see how they use interviewees from each category to tell a richer story.

You'll need a list many times longer than the number of interviews you hope to do - because for every 10 people you approach, 90% are likely to say no, ignore you, or say yes but never come through. So for a multi-interview feature with 3-6 interviewees you'll need to make 30-60 approaches.

## 5. Organise the interviews

Make as many approaches as you can to the potential interviewees, and start setting up interviews. Don't worry about having too many interviews because at least some will fall through, and having too much information is always better than having too little (you can always use material for more than one story).

Keep your initial approach short and simple: you want to interview them about X, and why (e.g. their expertise, experience, insights). Don't add the questions or promise anything in advance. This adds unnecessary detail and you want to retain as much independence as possible.

Try to use an approach with a higher success rate if possible, as follows:

1. Call their direct phone number: this provides the highest success rate. You should be able to find these for academic experts on university contact directories, and some company websites will list contact details for particular staff.
2. Indirect phone number, such as the general phone number for their department or organisation. You can call this and ask to be put through to them, or ask what their direct line or extension is.
3. Press/media office phone number: many organisations will have a phone number listed for their press office or media team. Call this and explain that you're looking for a quote from an expert on your topic. They will often chase the interviewee for you. This is a good approach for authors where their publisher should have a press office contact.
4. Direct email: email is easier to avoid and ignore, so make a call if you can, or combine the two approaches. Set a reminder to chase it up in a few days.
5. Press/media office email: as above.
6. Social media accounts: this has the lowest success rate. Not only is it extremely easy (and common) for people to ignore your message, but there's a good chance that messages are not even monitored very often, so they may not see it at all. There's also a higher chance that you are sending the message to the wrong person: an organisation's social media account is often maintained by a junior member of staff rather than someone who is able to put you in touch with an expert.

## 6. Plan the interviews

If you've done your background research properly then your interviews can focus only on what your interviewee knows, rather than basic facts you can find online. Make a list of questions that focus specifically on the areas your feature plans to tackle - why, how, what, and so on.

This is where knowing the themes of your feature makes a big difference: you can focus on the key questions that your feature is exploring.

In some cases you might want to conduct the interview in a significant place: the features above about Reform and Birmingham nightlife, for example, include interviews taken in key locations that add colour and other details to the story.

## 7. Conduct the interviews, including photos and other media

If you do conduct your interview in person, take photos so you have a record of what the place looks like, and notes of key details that might be useful for the story. Ask the interviewee if they would mind being photographed as well: it will make them more concrete for readers.

But even if you conduct the interview by phone or email, consider asking your interviewee for any relevant media (photos, audio clips, video footage) that might bring your story to life. And make notes about any non-verbal qualities of the interview: do they pause at certain points, or raise their voice, or become hesitant or gabble. Those might add colour, character, or key context (such as people being unsure or evasive).

## 8. Draft the story

As you complete interviews you can start to fill in the sections you planned for your story. Quotes from each interview can alternate with background research.

Identify any new questions that the interviews raise: you don't want your readers to be frustrated that there was a quote about something which was left hanging. Conduct background research to fill any gaps, or identify further interviews if you're going to need to take it in a new direction - or leave that element out of this piece, but make a note that it might be a good future story to follow up.

## 9. Edit the story

Allow yourself time to check the story for typos, factual accuracy, and structure. 

If anything isn't clear - such as a piece of jargon - then make sure you understand it. Contact an interviewee if there's any ambiguity or if you need to check a figure.

You should not show the final story to an interviewee before it is published: this compromises your editorial independence and makes it harder to maintain control of the story. You might show a section of a story (not the whole thing) to someone who is vulnerable and needs reassurance that they are not being exploited, but that is an exception. If anyone else asks for 'copy approval' (seeing the story before it's published), explain that you cannot do this (your editor and policy doesn't allow it) but that you can check specific quotes and facts: that should address concerns about being misquoted.

Be ruthless when you edit the story. You might have done a lot of research, but only the most important bits should make it into the story. Cut anything out that isn't essential (if in doubt, cut it and read to check if it still makes sense). You want to make it punchy and engaging.

## 10. Publish and distribute!

Once it's ready to go, don't just publish - think about how the story can be told across social media too, to bring in as wide an audience as possible.
