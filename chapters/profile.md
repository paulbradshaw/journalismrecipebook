# How to write a profile

A profile is a feature format that tells you what you need to know about a person or organisation - typically one that has recently been in the news. 

The pitch for a profile - whether it's a journalist suggesting it, or an editor asking a reporter to write one - is often something like "People will be wanting to know more about this person/organisation [because they are in the news]. Let's tell them what they need to know."

Profiles are similar to interviews but with the key difference that a profile is normally *not* based on an interview. Most profiles are based entirely on secondary research; some include quotes from people who know the person/organisation. And exceptional profiles will go even deeper. 

This recipe focuses on routine profiles based on secondary research ("desk research"), with more details on the other options at the end. Here are the steps:

1. Identify your target
2. List the questions to answer
3. Organise those into a profile structure
4. Do your background research
5. Fill your profile with the answers
6. Add an introduction and headline
7. Review and edit
8. Publish and distribute

**Before you begin a profile, look at examples to get a feel for the genre**:

* The Guardian collects all its profiles at https://www.theguardian.com/tone/profiles
* BBC profiles tend to start with "Who is ___". You can [find them using the search `site:bbc.co.uk/news "who is"`](https://www.google.com/search?q=site%3Abbc.co.uk%2Fnews+%22who+is%22)
* New Yorker Magazine has its profiles at https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/profiles


## 1. Identify your target (30 minutes)

Like any feature, you must be able to answer the question "Why would we publish this story now?"

For a profile, the answer is normally "Because this person/organisation is in the news right now and people want to know who/what they are".

Look at news stories coming out right now, or stories that you are working on, and look for opportunities for a profile: it might be a story that mentions someone that raises the question "Who is that person?" or it might be a story you're working on where readers might benefit from a link to a profile on a key individual (in which case you're getting two stories out of one).

There is a second reason why you might pitch a profile: "Because this person is really important but no one knows about them". This type of profile is more about 'shining a spotlight' on a person who is exercising power behind the scenes or away from the spotlight.

## 2. List the questions to answer (30 minutes)

Make a list of all the questions that you, and your audience, might ask about this person. 

Don't skimp on this process - you want to spend enough time that you get past the obvious, cliched ideas and list some original ones too. Use the 5 Ws and a H (who, what, where, why, when, and how) to help you brainstorm a wider range of questions (make sure you've got at least one for each).

Once you have at least 10-20 questions, you can start to select the best ones.

## 3. Organise those into a profile structure (30-60 minutes)

Pick questions from your list that will make a strong feature. Identify questions that are **practical** within the time you have to do the research.

Consider how those fit into a narrative structure. For example, you might organise the questions chronologically, and/or you might try to include the following:

* Have an 'orientation' question early on ('Why are they in the news right now?' is an obvious one to start with)
* Look for questions that introduce complications or tensions (and answers to those). Examples might include:
  * Concerns raised by or about the subject
  * Origins and obstacles faced 
  * Contradictions between image and reality (e.g. the politician who campaigns on austerity but has expensive tastes; the radical campaigner who turned establishment figure)
  * Journeys or transformations: how did this person become who they are? What changed them?
  * Disagreement, e.g. people who know them disagree sharply about what they’re really like.
  * Paradoxes in their work, e.g. famous but very private
* End with a 'coda' question/answer that looks ahead

A simple way to structure your profile is for each section to answer one question, with a subheading that introduces the question or part of the answer.

## 4. Research those questions (1-7 hours+)

The bulk of the work will be gathering the information to fill your profile. This is one of those jobs that can go on forever, so **set yourself a deadline** for each section and for the overall process. 

Remember that you are balancing two things: the need for **quality**, and the need for **speed**. Spend too long on the profile, and it will no longer be topical or relevant to your audience (if this is a topical feature). The objective is to tell your audience enough to be useful. 

Consider the following sources:

* Cuttings searches: Nexis (if you have access - students have access through their university library), Google News and your own publication's archives if you have one
* Podcasts and broadcast interviews: search for the person’s name on BBC Sounds, Spotify, or YouTube. These often contain more candid material than print interviews.
* Official websites and biographies: government profiles (gov.uk for politicians and public servants), company websites, university pages for academics, club pages for managers and athletes.
* Parliament’s website (parliament.uk): voting records, committee membership, maiden speeches and other parliamentary contributions for any MP or peer (what they say, how they vote)
* Companies House: directorships, filings, financial history for business figures.
* LinkedIn: career history for professionals; treat with some scepticism but useful as a starting point.
* Other social media accounts: what have they said and done in the past?
* Biographies on Who’s Who (if you have access through your employer or library) and Wikipedia (follow the references)
* Speeches: look for recordings or transcripts on websites for conferences and official websites, and YouTube
* Books or articles they have written, including blog posts
* Third-party perspectives: things said about your subject by current and former colleagues, friends and family, biographers, rivals and experts in their field

When you find a compelling quote in a secondary source, try to **trace it back to the original interview or speech** and check it is accurate.

**Keep track of your information in a master document or spreadsheet**. This should include the source of each fact or quote you might use in your article. Date every piece of information: things change, and you need to know whether a quote or claim is current.

## 5. Fill your profile with the answers

Once you have enough information for one of your sections, summarise it in a few paragraphs for that section in your profile.

Remember to be succinct: you're not just gathering information, but **selecting** the most important or interesting pieces, and leaving out the rest.

Repeat this process for each section in your article. Consider how one section leads to another: you might use a 'bridging' paragraph to make the transition smoother, i.e. touching on a question or event or theme that the next section will answer.

## 6. Add an introduction and headline

Once you have all the sections of your profile, you just need a beginning and a headline. 

Look at examples of profiles to see common patterns that are used,. 

For example, many profiles start by recapping the story that has prompted the profile: 

> "Ian Huntley, the former school caretaker who murdered two 10-year-old schoolgirls in Soham, has been attacked in prison." ([Who is Soham killer Ian Huntley?](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/c62dner722go))

> "The surprise appointment of BP’s third chief executive in a tumultuous five years reflects the embattled fossil fuel producer’s need for profound change. The hiring of Meg O’Neill appears to offer exactly that." ([Meg O’Neill: ‘hard-nosed’ outsider who will head BP’s pivot away from green energy](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2025/dec/18/meg-oneill-the-hard-nosed-boss-hired-to-be-the-new-head-of-bp))

Once current events have been summarised, you can move swiftly on to the information you've gathered about the subject: what is known or said about them, how they got here, what they have said and done, etc.

You should see recurring patterns in the headlines of profiles, too: 

* "Who is ____?" is a common pattern in BBC profiles
* Another common pattern is to raise a question, e.g. *[Authoritarians, strongmen and dictators: who is on Trump’s Board of Peace?](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/feb/19/authoritarians-strongmen-and-dictators-who-is-on-trumps-board-of-peace)*
* *Name: description* is used a lot by The Guardian, e.g. *[Hannah Spencer: the Green MP speaking to everyday people’s frustrations](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2026/feb/27/hannah-spencer-green-mp-gorton-and-denton-byelection-profile)* or *[Max Richter: the composer who crosses the invisible divide between ‘high’ and ‘low’ music](https://www.theguardian.com/music/2026/feb/06/max-richter-the-composer-who-crosses-the-invisible-divide-between-high-and-low-music)*
* *Quote: description* is another, e.g. *[‘The real ringleader’: the Venezuelan security chief with a $25m bounty on his head](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/jan/13/venezuelan-security-chief-diosdado-cabello-profile)* or *[‘It’s about ego’: Matt Goodwin’s journey from far-right expert to firebrand Reform candidate](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2026/jan/30/matt-goodwin-academic-gb-news-presenter-reform-candidate)*

## 7. Review and edit

Don't forget to review your draft and edit it ruthlessly by following this checklist:

* Edit for length: decide how many words your subject justifies. Will an audience really want to spend an hour reading about them, or 10 minutes? Cut to that length. If you haven't cut anything yet, you haven't done your job. A 'who is' profile will typically be around 500 words; a more biographical profile might be 800-1200 words.
* Edit for style: make sure it fits the style of your target publication (check their style guide) and their audience (is the language at the right level?)
* Edit for clarity: check for jargon and acronyms and make sure they are translated into simple language and/or explained
* Edit for accuracy: identify every factual claim in the story and check it is backed up by your research. Make sure it is clearly attributed and any context or caveats included (for example you might specify "speaking in 2018" to make the date context clear)
* Edit for legal and ethical considerations: as with any article, identify any potential legal or ethical issues including defamation, copyright, contempt, use of inappropriate language, etc. Discuss and address those with colleagues or lecturers.

## 8. Publish and distribute

Once you're confident the article is good enough to publish, don't forget to create updates for social media, too. Re-tell the story for each platform rather than 'promoting' it. For example you might create a visual version of five key facts from the story for Instagram, a thread for X, or an emoji-bullet list for LinkedIn or Facebook. 

## Taking it up a level

Once you feel confident with a standard profile based on desk research, you might want to try something more ambitious. 

One way to do this is to include original research alongside the secondary research - by **interviewing** people who know your subject, rather than only using things already published. 

You might also get exclusive information through **documentary evidence**: for example you might use FOI to obtain correspondence by or about your subject, or use sources to obtain images, video or audio. 

**Data analysis** is a third technique: this might be analysis of their social media activity or connections, or themes in their speeches. 

In each of these cases **the subject must justify the time involved**. Original research or analysis will slow down production, too, so it will need to still be topical.
