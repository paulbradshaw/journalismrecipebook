# How to write a reaction story

![](https://github.com/paulbradshaw/journalismrecipebook/blob/main/images/Follow-on%20ideas%20from%20news%20events.png?raw=true)

A common way to get a news story is to look for newsworthy **reaction** to another news story. This reaction is part of the **news cycle**, where each story stimulates reaction and action, each of which becomes a story in its own right.

Check out the following examples to get an idea of what a reaction story looks like, and how it fits into a news cycle:

* [Waitrose under pressure to reinstate worker sacked after stopping shoplifter](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2026/apr/06/waitrose-employee-sacked-stopping-shoplifter-walker-smith-easter-egg) follows a [previous story on the worker being sacked](Waitrose employee sacked after stopping shoplifter from taking Easter eggs)
* [Sexual assault survivor calls failure of Travelodge boss to meet MPs ‘shocking’](https://www.theguardian.com/society/2026/mar/30/sexual-assault-survivor-calls-failure-travelodge-boss-meet-mps-shocking) reports on a reaction to a story four days earlier - [Starmer tells Travelodge boss to engage with MPs over sexual assault case](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2026/mar/26/starmer-tells-travelodge-boss-to-engage-with-mps-over-sexual-assault-case), which itself was the latest development in an ongoing news story.

The key thing with a reaction story is that the reaction must be **newsworthy**: it must come from someone whose comments are significant for some reason. 

The [PEER model](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2026/03/09/peer-a-technique-for-brainstorming-interviewees-and-story-sources/) (Power, Expertise, Experience, Representatives) provides a useful method for identifying whose reactions are likely to be newsworthy:

* Powerful people's reactions are newsworthy because they can do something about the event, or cause others to do something. In the examples above, reactions from the shadow home secretary, Chris Philp (political power) and broadcaster Iain Dale (cultural power) are both in that category.
* Expert reactions are newsworthy because of their expertise and authority (which again can cause those in power to do something).
* Representatives are newsworthy because they represent a wider group of people. Journalists will often seek out quotes from these in reaction to proposals for new laws or rules which will affect people in a particular industry (e.g. asking unions or industry bodies), or are supposed to help a particular group of people (e.g. asking charities or campaign groups).
* People who have experience of the event or type of event are less likely to be the entire basis of a reaction story, because their voice carries less influence than the other categories, but they might be one of the reaction quotes alongside the other categories. An exception is people who were directly involved in the initial event, which is the case with the Travelodge example above: the story leads on the reaction that she "has said she was shocked to learn the hotel chain’s boss cancelled a meeting with a group of MPs seeking to discuss concerns about the case."

Note that many reaction stories are based on identifying public reaction: the assault victim was talking on ITV’s Good Morning Britain; Iain Dale on his LBC radio show; and Chris Philp posted his letter on social media.  

## The recipe

1. Identify a news story which has follow-up potential
2. Make a list of potential interviewees
3. Decide on a core question
4. Make the approaches
5. Write up the story

## 1. Identify a news story which has follow-up potential

For this story you need an initial news event which you will follow up on. Multiple news stories published and broadcast every day will provide opportunities to do this, so the key is to read, listen to and watch news output and look out for stories which you can follow up. The best time to do this is first thing in the day, while news is still fresh.

One example many journalists follow is [Radio 4's Today programme](https://www.bbc.co.uk/sounds/play/live/bbc_radio_fourfm), which is on air from 6am-9am and often sets the news agenda for the day ahead, particularly through its interviews. 

Similarly, many broadcast journalists will start the day by looking at what the daily newspapers have reported, and following up on those.

Later in the day you might check again for any new stories that have broken, or any developments on the story you were following up on. Freshness is key.

## 2. List potential interviewees

Again, [use the PEER model](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2026/03/09/peer-a-technique-for-brainstorming-interviewees-and-story-sources/) (Power, Expertise, Experience, Representatives) to brainstorm potential types of interviewee you could get a newsworthy reaction from.

**Representatives** are the best place to look if the news event is likely to affect a particular group of people. Identify what professional bodies, unions, specialist media and other organisations exist to represent people related to your story. If it relates to health issues, look for charities representing people with that condition. If it affects a geographical area, you might make a list of representatives of people in that area: MPs and councillors are just two examples.

**Power** is the second most likely source type: who has the power to make or enforce rules? Spend money? Cultural influence? 

**Experts** are less often quoted, but can also give you a useful angle if they raise concerns or warnings. Look for people who have expertise on the types of events this story relates to, not just academics who have researched the field (try Google Scholar), but those who have written books about it (with major publishers), those who have won awards or success in the field, and those operating in the field.

**Experience**-related sources would include those who witnessed or experienced any initial news event, or were quoted in previous news stories about similar events. A cuttings search is best for this (use Nexis to look for mentions of key words in news reports), but you might also be able to find people through charities or campaign groups - as long as their experience is significant enough to give their reaction authority.

Some of your research into potential interviews will give you useful background research that helps to flesh out the story and establish who the person is and why their reaction is newsworthy.

Make sure your list of potential interviewees has at least 20 names on it, and at least 15 of those are representatives or in roles of power. With a 10% success rate that should give you two actual interviews. 

## 3. Decide on a core question

**Decide what you need from the interviewee**. You'll need to know this before you make an approach, because it will make it easier for you to get a response (the source will know more clearly what you want from them, and can provide it more quickly). 

Your question is likely to be one of the following:

* What needs to be done? 
* What concerns do they have?
* What potential problems would they warn about? (Experts are particularly good for this)
* *For those with direct experience of an initial incident only*: how do they feel about the latest development? (This is the lead angle of *Sexual assault survivor calls failure of Travelodge boss to meet MPs ‘shocking’* but feelings won't be newsworthy enough for anyone else. Focus instead on concrete concerns/warnings etc.)
* *For representatives*: what are their members saying/calling for/concerned about/warning of?
* *For representatives*: What are they telling members? 

**Find any answers to your questions that are already public**: you don't want to waste an interview getting information that you could have found through a simple Google search. Your interviewee will be insulted that you've wasted their time, and will think you are lazy, making them less likely to cooperate in future.

Find the social media accounts for the interviewee, their organisation (and any news releases page where they might issue statements). You might find they've already commented on the event and you can either quote from that, or follow up on it for something original.

## 4. Make the approaches - and chase up

Start finding contact details and making approaches - because of the short timescale, phonecalls are always better. If you can't find a direct contact detail, call the switchboard at their organisation or ask someone connected with them if they can put you in touch. As always, be professional, empathetic, and genuinely interested in them (open your email by explaining that you're reporting on the particular story .

Because it's only one question you're unlikely to need to organise a full interview: a phonecall or email will be enough to get the quotes you need. 

In this scenario you wouldn't say "I'm looking for an interview", you'd say "I'm reporting on responses to...", or "I'm looking for a response to" or "I'm looking for a reaction to", etc. This makes it clear that your demands are simple.

Tell them the question you are seeking an answer to in your first email, e.g. after that initial context, say "What concerns do you have about..?"

Don't forget to give a clear indication of timeline in your email (e.g. "I'm aiming to complete the story by the end of today") and chase up after an hour or two: this story will only be newsworthy for a limited time and chasing up after a week wouldn't make sense in most cases.

*Note: a full interview is also an option, as it might give you more stories alongside the initial one you were planning (including a full feature exploring the issue). This will depend on timescale, however.*

## 5. Write up the story

As soon as you get one reaction, you can start to put together the story. Because this is a news story, it should follow the inverted pyramid structure, with the newest piece of information at the top, followed by information in order of importance, like so:

* Headline: Concerns or warnings are being raised, problems highlighted, pressure being applied ("Waitrose under pressure to reinstate worker sacked after stopping shoplifter")
* 1st par: repeats same info from headline, with a few extra words ("Waitrose is under growing pressure to reinstate an employee of 17 years who was sacked after tackling a shoplifter who was trying to steal Lindt Gold Bunny Easter eggs.")
* Pars 2+: provide some background to the story: what happened, when/where, etc. ("The retailer has faced public outcry over its treatment of Walker Smith, who was fired two days after he stopped the shoplifter taking items from the Easter egg display.")
* Later pars: get into the particular reaction quotes as early as possible, ideally around the 3rd or 4th par, then weave in extra pars of background context to break up further quotes
* End pars: quotes that look ahead, or facts about what is planned to happen next can be good ways to end. You might also end on a response or no comment from someone responsible, or a final quote from the case study if they appeared early in the story. The Waitrose story, for example, ends on a response from Waitrose.

## Other examples to use as templates and guides

* [Family of one-punch attack victim fear £500k compensation could run out](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cy91p4qzn24o) is a more broadcast-style reaction story following up on a previous story that "new figures from campaigners suggest that had the maximum amount risen in line with inflation, it would stand at more than double the current limit – at £1,015,000."
* [Holiday let owner says legislation will 'destroy me'](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cly6826p8j8o) reports on reaction to "rules on holiday lets introduced by the Welsh government three years ago."
