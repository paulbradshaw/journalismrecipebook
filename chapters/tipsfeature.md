# How to do a multi-interview tips feature

Tips features provide practical advice on a particular problem or practice. They can be based entirely on secondary research, on a single expert interview, or on multiple interviews.

The multi-interview tips feature has a number of advantages: it can be punchy, have a lot of variety, and be packed with useful advice.

This story format is also a great way to build your contacts book and improve the chances of getting responses from interviewees.

Two examples are: 

* *[All the right moves! 17 personal trainers on the exercise they always recommend – from planks to face pulls](https://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/2026/apr/29/17-personal-trainers-exercise-they-always-recommend-planks-face-pulls)*
* *[The best £20 you’ll ever spend: 16 affordable essentials the experts swear by)](https://www.theguardian.com/money/article/2024/aug/23/16-affordable-essentials-under-20-pounds-chosen-by-experts)* 

Here's the recipe:

1. Choose a problem or skill that people will need tips for
2. Brainstorm a list of 'experts'
3. Shortlist so you have a good variety
4. Do any background research needed
5. Find contact details and make approaches 
6. Select your quotes and organise them into sections
7. Write an intro
8. Find images to illustrate
9. Publish and distribute


## 1. Choose a problem or skill that people will need tips for - and express it as a simple question

"Can you buy anything useful and good quality for £20 or under?" That's the problem that *[The best £20 you’ll ever spend: 16 affordable essentials the experts swear by)](https://www.theguardian.com/money/article/2024/aug/23/16-affordable-essentials-under-20-pounds-chosen-by-experts)* tackles. "What's the one essential exercise?" Is the question that *[All the right moves! 17 personal trainers on the exercise they always recommend – from planks to face pulls](https://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/2026/apr/29/17-personal-trainers-exercise-they-always-recommend-planks-face-pulls)* sets out to answer.

Your feature will need a similar simple question that you can pose to your experts. 

Start with a problem that people often need to solve, or a skill that is widely practised. Skills are easier: there are endless hobbies, sports and other pastimes that you could choose. Problems will give you a more original feature, however, so it's worth the extra effort. Problems might not be **practical** - they could be **social** (e.g. dealing with a particular type of person or situation) or **psychological** (e.g. motivation).

Keep your eye out for problems expressed by friends, family and colleagues, and mentioned in the media. Or start with a common practice or skill and brainstorm problems within that. For example, when people are cooking they often encounter particular problems, and different people might take up exercise to tackle different problems (e.g. weight, mental health, injury, medical condition, meeting a partner etc). Speak to people about their hobbies and interests to find out why they took up those activities, or what problems they regularly encounter.


## 2. Brainstorm a list of 'experts'

A good tips feature needs people with expertise in the field - but expertise can take a range of forms. In my [PEER framework](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2026/03/09/peer-a-technique-for-brainstorming-interviewees-and-story-sources/) I describe a number of types of expertise: academic; research expertise outside of academia (e.g. statisticians, charity researchers and analysts); authors; professional expertise (senior positions and/or long service); the expertise of achievement (e.g. award-winning); and the expertise of repeated experience (for example someone who has patented dozens of inventions).

Use that list to try to brainstorm ideas for experts: first, **decide which categories are relevant and what shape they take for your story**. 

For example in a story about exercise it might be authors (of fitness books), academics (who've researched exercise), award-winners (athletes and trainers) and professionals (experienced trainers). A story about dealing with bullies, however, might draw advice from senior professionals (who've had to manage bullies), academic experts (e.g. psychologists), and authors (of books on the topic). A story about getting a book published might only need tips from people with repeated experience (they've pitched multiple books, successfully).

Write down each category you've chosen and try to come up with 3-5 names. Where you cannot come up with names, write down 'types' of sources, e.g. *"Someone who has written a book about bullying among children"*, *"Something who has written a book about bullying at work"*, and so on. The aim is to have a list which is more specific and varied than just 'an author' or 'a professional'.

For example if you were going a story about exercising tips for people who've had a heart attack, you might list: academic researchers on recovering from heart attacks; academic researchers on cardio exercise; trainers who specialise in this field; trainers who have had a heart attack themselves; an author who's written a book on getting back to fitness after a heart attack; and so on.

Remember: *brainstorming is about quantity, not quality*, so you should not be saying "that's a bad idea" at this stage. Just get as many ideas down as possible and try not to judge or edit what you're doing. There will be a moment when you're struggling to think of more ideas - don't stop. The key thing is to *keep going* past that point, because this is when you've written down all the obvious, cliched ideas, and it's *after* that moment when you will come up with more original and interesting ones. 

I've seen this described as like running an old disused tap: at first the water is muddy, so you have to run it for a while to flush out the poor quality water and get the better stuff.
