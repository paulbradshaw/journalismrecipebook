# How to do a multi-interview tips feature

Tips features provide practical advice on a particular problem or practice. They can be based entirely on secondary research, on a single expert interview, or on multiple interviews.

The multi-interview tips feature has a number of advantages: it can be punchy, have a lot of variety, and be packed with useful advice.

This story format is also a great way to build your contacts book and improve the chances of getting responses from interviewees.

Two examples are: 

* *[All the right moves! 17 personal trainers on the exercise they always recommend – from planks to face pulls](https://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/2026/apr/29/17-personal-trainers-exercise-they-always-recommend-planks-face-pulls)*
* *[The best £20 you’ll ever spend: 16 affordable essentials the experts swear by)](https://www.theguardian.com/money/article/2024/aug/23/16-affordable-essentials-under-20-pounds-chosen-by-experts)* 

Here's the recipe:

1. Choose a problem or skill that people will need tips for
2. Do any background research needed
3. Brainstorm a list of 'experts'
4. Find contact details and make approaches 
5. Select your quotes and organise them into sections
6. Write an intro
7. Find images to illustrate
8. Publish and distribute


## 1. Choose a problem or skill that people will need tips for - and express it as a simple question

"Can you buy anything useful and good quality for £20 or under?" That's the problem that *[The best £20 you’ll ever spend: 16 affordable essentials the experts swear by)](https://www.theguardian.com/money/article/2024/aug/23/16-affordable-essentials-under-20-pounds-chosen-by-experts)* tackles. "What's the one essential exercise?" Is the question that *[All the right moves! 17 personal trainers on the exercise they always recommend – from planks to face pulls](https://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/2026/apr/29/17-personal-trainers-exercise-they-always-recommend-planks-face-pulls)* sets out to answer.

Your feature will need a similar simple question that you can pose to your experts. 

Start with a problem that people often need to solve, or a skill that is widely practised. Skills are easier: there are endless hobbies, sports and other pastimes that you could choose. Problems will give you a more original feature, however, so it's worth the extra effort. Problems might not be **practical** - they could be **social** (e.g. dealing with a particular type of person or situation) or **psychological** (e.g. motivation).

Keep your eye out for problems expressed by friends, family and colleagues, and mentioned in the media. Or start with a common practice or skill and brainstorm problems within that. For example, when people are cooking they often encounter particular problems, and different people might take up exercise to tackle different problems (e.g. weight, mental health, injury, medical condition, meeting a partner etc). Speak to people about their hobbies and interests to find out why they took up those activities, or what problems they regularly encounter.

## 2. Do a little background research

The better your understanding of the problem, the easier it will be to generate ideas around it (including interviewees, the next step), so you will save time further down the line by doing a little bit of background research at the start of the process.

The first thing to check is if anyone has written a story like yours before. This helps in three ways: it gives you a model to follow, it gives you more ideas, and avoids you doing something that was already done *exactly the same way recently*. 

It's fine to do a story which is *similar* to a previous feature, but you want to make sure that your story is distinctive: it might be more up to date, for example (if the previous story was done a few years ago), or use different experts, or you might need to tweak your idea slightly to focus on a slightly different question.

Apart from that, read enough about the subject so that you are familiar with any jargon that's used, and you have an idea of the types of people in that field (e.g. who are the professionals? What types of awards might people win? What sort of research might be done?) You should also spot people who've been interviewed in other stories, that you could interview for yours.

## 3. Brainstorm a list of 'experts'

A good tips feature needs people with expertise in the field - but expertise can take a range of forms. In my [PEER framework](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2026/03/09/peer-a-technique-for-brainstorming-interviewees-and-story-sources/) I describe a number of types of expertise: academic; research expertise outside of academia (e.g. statisticians, charity researchers and analysts); authors; professional expertise (senior positions and/or long service); the expertise of achievement (e.g. award-winning); and the expertise of *repeated* experience (for example someone who has patented dozens of inventions, but not someone who has only patented one).

**Note: an expert has to have some sort of authority on the topic. Someone who is studying at university, for example, is not an expert on their subject. A volunteer, personal trainer, part time employee etc. is not an expert unless they've been doing the role for over a decade.**

Use that list to try to brainstorm ideas for experts: first, **decide which categories are relevant and what shape they take for your story**. 

For example in a story about exercise it might be authors (of fitness books), academics (who've researched exercise), award-winners (athletes and trainers) and professionals (experienced trainers). A story about dealing with bullies, however, might draw advice from senior professionals (who've had to manage bullies), academic experts (e.g. psychologists), and authors (of books on the topic). A story about getting a book published might only need tips from people with repeated experience (they've pitched multiple books, successfully).

Write down each category you've chosen and try to come up with 3-5 names. Where you cannot come up with names, write down 'types' of sources, e.g. *"Someone who has written a book about bullying among children"*, *"Something who has written a book about bullying at work"*, and so on. The aim is to have a list which is more specific and varied than just 'an author' or 'a professional'.

For example if you were going a story about exercising tips for people who've had a heart attack, you might list: academic researchers on recovering from heart attacks; academic researchers on cardio exercise; trainers who specialise in this field; trainers who have had a heart attack themselves; an author who's written a book on getting back to fitness after a heart attack; and so on.

Remember: *brainstorming is about quantity, not quality*, so you should not be saying "that's a bad idea" at this stage. Just get as many ideas down as possible and try not to judge or edit what you're doing. There will be a moment when you're struggling to think of more ideas - don't stop. The key thing is to *keep going* past that point, because this is when you've written down all the obvious, cliched ideas, and it's *after* that moment when you will come up with more original and interesting ones. 

I've seen this described as like running an old disused tap: at first the water is muddy, so you have to run it for a while to flush out the poor quality water and get the better stuff.

## 4. Find contact details and make approaches 

Now start to put names and contact details against the list you made. The contact details should be prioritised in the following order:

1. Direct phone number: this provides the highest success rate. You should be able to find these for academic experts on university contact directories, and some company websites will list contact details for particular staff.
2. Indirect phone number: this also has a high success rate. Look for the general phone number for their department or organisation. You can call this and ask to be put through to them, or ask what their direct line or extension is.
3. Press/media office phone number: many organisations will have a phone number listed for their press office or media team. Call this and explain that you're looking for a quote from an expert on your topic. They will often chase the interviewee for you. This is a good approach for authors where their publisher should have a press office contact.
4. Direct email: email is easier to avoid and ignore, so make a call if you can, or combine the two approaches. Set a reminder to chase it up in a few days.
5. Press/media office email: as above.
6. Social media accounts: this has the lowest success rate. Not only is it extremely easy (and common) for people to ignore your message, but there's a good chance that messages are not even monitored very often, so they may not see it at all. There's also a higher chance that you are sending the message to the wrong person: an organisation's social media account is often maintained by a junior member of staff rather than someone who is able to put you in touch with an expert.

You will need enough names to be able to get 15-20 quotes for your story. Aim to have 30-50 names to contact in order to get these. 

That might sound a lot, but it's actually not many for a good piece of journalism. In most stories you need about 10 names for every successful approach (1 success for every 10 approaches), but in this format we are not making big demands on the interviewee, so the success rate should be higher. 

When you make contact, explain your feature succinctly, and explain why you think they'd be perfect for the piece: it helps to show you know something about them, and give them a reason to participate. Make it clear you're just asking for one tip. This clarifies that they don't need to do an interview, but only think of one thing to send quickly.

Set a reminder to chase people up after a week. If you don't have enough responses by that time, chase up the ones you've contacted, and contact more experts as well (try different types), as a low success rate means you will need to contact more people to get one successful response. Review your approach, too (get feedback): it might be that your email is too long or short, or you can more clearly pitch the story, etc.
