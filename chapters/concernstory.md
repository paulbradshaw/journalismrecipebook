# How to: use a trend to write a news story based on 'concerns' or 'warnings'

A common way to find a news story is to identify a trend and report on any concerns about that. An example of this approach can be found in the Sarah Marsh story *[‘Wild west’ reformer pilates boom is causing rise in injuries, experts warn](https://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/2026/apr/04/reformer-pilates-boom-injuries)*.

Trends - things growing quickly or new fashions - are often newsworthy because they represent change and because fast growth and new practices often bring new challenges and new problems.

Here's how to do it:

1. Identify the trend
2. Research and list potential interviewees
3. Prepare for the interviews
4. Make the approaches
5. Organise and conduct the interview
6. Write up the story

## 1. Identify the trend

Keep your eyes and ears open for things that appear to be growing, or completely new. It might be things that you notice people talking about, or being advertised, or it might be trends identified in reports or data (Google Trends is a good place to look).

## 2. Research and list potential interviewees

The trend is not your story - it's the *reaction* to that trend that is the new thing in your story. [Use the PEER model](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2026/03/09/peer-a-technique-for-brainstorming-interviewees-and-story-sources/) (Power, Expertise, Experience, Representatives) to brainstorm potential interviewees of four different types.

Do some background research to help with your brainstorming, along these lines:

**Experts**: who has expertise in the areas likely to be affected by this trend? Look for academics who have researched the field (try Google Scholar), those who have written books about it (with major publishers), and those who have won awards or success in the field.

**Representatives**: the key to Sarah Marsh's story is quotes from representatives of those involved in the industry: the director of the Society for the Pilates Method (SPM), and The Pilates Teacher Association. Identify what professional bodies, unions, specialist media and other organisations exist to represent people related to your story. 

**Experience**: who has been affected by the trend? Marsh's story quotes Kirsty Morgan, a pilates teacher who was injured herself, and Sarah-Jane Walls, a "qualified podiatrist and pilates teacher" who says she had seen “trauma injuries” and people falling off machines doing “something unsafe”. Look for people with experiences by looking for groups or hashtags where people are likely to share experiences, by asking around in relevant circles, including relevant charities and campaign groups (who can act as go-betweens), and through a cuttings search (use Nexis to look for mentions of key words in news reports). 

**Power**: who has the power to make or enforce rules? Spend money? Cultural influence? In Marsh's story we have EMD UK, the "national governing body for group exercise", and Philippa Wheeler, a solicitor at the law firm Leigh Day, which was "investigating a claim against a well-known retailer in relation to a reformer pilates machine sold in their stores".

Some of your research into potential interviews will give you information you can use in the story. For example, Marsh's story includes this detail: 

> "Maya Meron, an acclaimed violinist, was injured at a London pilates studio when a Coreformer – a proprietary reformer machine – collapsed, breaking her left elbow and causing abdominal injuries that ended her performing career. She sued the studio and the court ruled largely in her favour."

This is not an interviewee (she probably tried to get an interview but wasn't successful), but it is still useful background research that helps to flesh out the story.

## 3. Prepare for the interviews

Make sure your list of potential interviewees has at least 20 names on it - with a 10% success rate that should give you two actual interviews. (Note that Marsh's story is an unusually long news story because she has a good number of interviews.)

**Decide what you need from each interview**. You'll need to know this before you make an approach, because it will make it easier for you to get a response (the source will know more clearly what you want from them, and can provide it more quickly). 

Typically each type of source plays a particular role in your story, from engaging the reader in the issue by demonstrating its impact, to explaining how widespread it is, to exploring what is being done or needs to be done, or what might happen next. Here are some typical questions to consider:

* Power: what are they doing? What needs to be done? Why isn't action being taken? What concerns do they have?
* Expertise: how big is the issue? How much is it growing/falling? Is it getting better or worse? What concerns do they have?
* Experience: what is it like to experience the issue? What problems has it caused? What obstacles do they face? What concerns do they have?
* Representatives: what are their members saying? What are they telling members? What concerns do they have?

The "What concerns do they have?" question is central - it is what the story rests on, so at least one source needs to answer that question. Experts are best positioned to raise concerns because of their independence and knowledge, and representatives because they represent the concerns of a group of people. 

**Find any answers to your questions that are already public**: you don't want to waste an interview getting information that you could have found through a simple Google search. Your interviewee will be insulted that you've wasted their time, and will think you are lazy, making them less likely to cooperate in future.

For example: look for information about the scale of the issue and how much it's changing; what is being done about the issue; concerns that have already been raised; and so on.

You will also want to answer simple background questions which you will need to answer in your story. In Marsh's story, for example, there's some basic background on Pilates itself, which "was developed in the early 20th century by Joseph Pilates". Again, this is information you should have *before* you approach interviewees, to avoid looking ignorant or lazy.

## 4. Make the approaches - and chase up

You can choose to either ask for an interview, a chat, or for a quote. Asking for a quote is more convenient but also more transactional/impersonal, and you will get less material from it. This is best for people used to dealing with the media such as representatives and those in power, who might also have less time for a full chat.  

Asking for an interview or a chat shows more interest in the person and is better for building trust. It will also give you more material to work with, but it is more time-consuming for both of you. This is best for experts and case studies. 

Your preparation in step 3 above should give you an idea whether you have a lot of questions that will need a chat/interview, or just one or two questions which can be answered through a simple email asking for a quote in response to those (this is why preparation can make a lot of difference to your success rate).

Remember that **the less (newsworthy) material you get from interviews, the more interviews you need**. Marsh's story draws on multiple interviews, but doesn't suggest any of them were in-depth interviews (this may not be the case, though). A different way of doing the story would have been one or two more detailed interviews, with more quotes from them.

Once you've got a long enough list, start finding contact details and making approaches. If you can't find a direct contact detail, call the switchboard at their organisation or ask someone connected with them if they can put you in touch. As always, be professional, empathetic, and genuinely interested in them.

Don't forget to give a clear indication of timeline in your email (e.g. "I'm aiming to complete the story by Friday") and set a reminder in your calendar to chase up after a certain period. 

## 6. Organise and conduct any interviews

If you are conducting interviews (and not just getting quotes), organise them at the interviewee's convenience (but try to organise them in the morning as developments at work are more likely to lead to cancellations in the afternoon). 

If you are lucky enough to get more interviews than you needed, don't turn any of them down - do all the interviews, treating the later ones as backups in case the earlier interviews cancel, and using all interviews as opportunities for other stories (any interview can be turned into a story). 

When you conduct the interview make sure you don't just read out the questions you've prepared: it's vital to listen actively to the interviewee and what they tell you. Consider whether you need to ask them to expand on their response further, or if it raises any more questions you want to follow up with.

Record the interview and make notes as well. It helps to include timestamps on the notes so you can locate the part of the recording it relates to, if you need to check what they said or fill in blanks you didn't get down.

## 7. Write up the story

As you gather material, start to put it into story format. Because this is a news story, it should follow the inverted pyramid structure, with the newest piece of information at the top, followed by information in order of importance, like so:

* Headline: Concerns or warnings are being raised, or problems highlighted (the example goes with "‘Wild west’ reformer pilates boom is causing rise in injuries, experts warn")
* 1st par: repeats same info from headline, with a few extra words ("The boom in reformer pilates has created a “wild west” of studios where poor regulation has resulted in inexperienced teachers and a rise in injuries, professional standards bodies have warned.")
* Pars 2+: provide some background to the story: what is happening or has happened recently, when/where, etc. A longer story with more quotes can have more background including anecotes; a shorter story might only provide background on a key event and the people being quoted
* Later pars: get into quotes as early as possible, ideally around the 3rd or 4th par, then weave in extra pars of background context to break up further quotes
* End pars: quotes that look ahead, or facts about what is planned to happen next can be good ways to end. You might also end on a response or no comment from someone responsible, or a final quote from the case study if they appeared early in the story.
