# How to write... an anniversary feature

News organisations regularly use anniversaries as a news 'hook' to hang a story on and plan around. Here are some examples:

* [One year on: how landmark ruling on single-sex spaces has changed lives](https://www.theguardian.com/society/2026/apr/15/how-scotland-landmark-ruling-single-sex-spaces-changed-lives) uses the one year anniversary of a supreme court judgment
* In politics and war, 100 days is often used as a key moment to review what has happened, as in [Never mind the flag bans, where are the policies? Assessing 100 days of Reform-led councils](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/ng-interactive/2025/aug/30/100-days-of-reform-led-councils-flag-bans-policies) and [100 days of war in Ukraine: The devastating consequences of Putin's invasion](https://news.sky.com/story/100-days-of-war-in-ukraine-the-devastating-consequences-of-putins-invasion-12626510)
* A decade on a major sporting event is a good opportunity to look back at its legacy: [‘A Jerusalem for everyone’: was the 2012 Olympics the last gasp of liberal Britain?](https://www.theguardian.com/culture/2022/jul/16/danny-boyle-2012-london-olympic-opening-ceremony-feelgood-factor?CMP=insidesaturday_email)
* Fashion editor Jess Cartner-Morley uses an anniversary to explore why one shopping mall is bucking the trend: [Bicester Village at 30: how a wet field in Oxfordshire defied the death of the shopping mall](https://www.theguardian.com/fashion/2025/may/10/bicester-village-30-oxfordshire-shopping-mall)
* Even 35 years can be a round enough number, as in [Miners' strike: The decades-old feud that still divides communities](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-england-nottinghamshire-47401859)
* 100 years is a big anniversary. Stories like [Spanish flu: 'We didn't know who we'd lose next'](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-england-45097068) rely on interviews with experts, documents and data

Anniversary stories are most often written as features - but it's worth mentioning that they can also act as a hook for a news story (e.g. "A year on from a new law, new data reveals no one has been charged") and liveblogs ([EFL Rewind: Relive the opening day of Football League action 130 years after it happened](https://www.bbc.co.uk/sport/live/football/45201526)).

This recipe, however, focuses on anniversary *features*.

1. Identify, or plan for, the anniversary of a major news event
2. Brainstorm story angles - and choose one
3. Do any background research needed
4. Identify a range of potential interviewees
5. Conduct the interviews
6. Edit the results into a story
7. Distribute

## 1. Identify, or plan for, the anniversary of a major event

Try one of the following:

* When a major news event occurs, make a diary note to start revisiting it in 11 months' time for its one-year anniversary.
* For any forthcoming dates, look up any potential anniversaries - there are lots of websites that list these. Search for 'forthcoming anniversaries' and find a page that works or check Wikipedia's anniversaries page by editing the URL https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Selected_anniversaries/July_7 to point to any date you choose
* Search for key events in an area of interest that you can 'hook' a story on.

For example, if you are interested in a sport like rugby league, there will be anniversaries of the founding of each team, league, competition, stadium and significant result (like a cup win or loss), anniversaries of the introduction of new rules, of significant events like a legal case or notable injury or sponsorship deal or appointment or sacking. 

If you are interested in a current affairs topic like homelessness, there will be anniversaries of laws, guidelines and policies being introduced, charities and bodies being founded, reports or data being released, or any other news events around the issue. (*Tip: you can make another story out of your background research by creating a timeline of key events in that field, or a listicle of 'Ten key events in X' and publishing that*)

## 2. Brainstorm story angles - and choose one

Looking at those previous events will give you ideas for stories. You might notice it's been a decade since a club last won a trophy, or five years since a new policy was introduced to reduce rough sleeping, or a year since police were criticised for stopping and searching a successful British athlete. Each of those provides a potential 'hook' for a story exploring whether things have improved, or if the policy worked, or why the club has failed to win anything.

Make a list of all the potential angles you could focus on. Remember that your story needs to have a clear focus, so once you have a list you will have to pick just one. Try to cover the following types of questions:

* "Why" something happened/happens: now that some time has passed, people in the field should know more
* "How" something happened/happens: this angle focuses more on revisiting the event and breaking down what we now know about what happened
* "What" has happened since: for negative events, you might focus on what has been done since to stop it happening again
* "Has anything changed?" Again, for negative events, exploring *if* the causes have been tackled
* "What has changed?" A similar approach but more focused on anniversaries of new policies or processes
* "What is the legacy?" Particularly useful for events that were supposed to leave a legacy, either good or bad (e.g. Olympics, Brexit) 
* "What have we learned?" 
* "Is it working?" is a useful angle for anniversaries of new initiatives such as new laws, new policies, new guidelines, new practices etc. that were introduced with the intention of achieving a particular results such as reducing something bad (like crime, etc.) or increasing something good (like revenue, fans etc)
* How does it connect with current events? Sometimes past events feel particularly relevant to current ones. It might be obvious, like what the anniversary of the launch of a league tells us at a time when a new league is being proposed, or it might be more indirect, like how the anniversary of a major protest mirrors current debates around free speech.
